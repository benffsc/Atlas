## Strategic Overview

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "FFSC has helped over 42,000 cats across Sonoma County - that's remarkable for a grassroots organization. Our overall alteration rate is 84%, which is well above the 70% threshold researchers consider necessary to stabilize a cat population. In other words, we're not just helping individual cats - we're actually changing the trajectory of the community cat population in Sonoma County."

## Current Priorities

### 8. "What location needs the most attention right now?"

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "Looking at active requests, 36855 Annapolis Road stands out - the caretaker reported 45 cats but we've only verified 22. That gap means there are probably 20+ cats still unfixed and waiting. Armstrong Woods Road is similar - 30 reported, only 5 verified.

> Now, you might see 1688 Jennings Way with 187 cats and a 5.9% rate and think that's urgent. But I should be honest about the data here - most of those 187 cats have unknown status, not confirmed unaltered. It's legacy data where we didn't capture the alteration status. So the real priority is the active requests where we KNOW there are unfixed cats waiting."

---

### 9. "Are there any active requests where we know there are more cats than we've trapped?"

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "Yes - this is exactly what I look for when prioritizing. At 36855 Annapolis Road, someone reported 45 cats but we've only processed 22 through the clinic. That's a clear signal that there's more work to do. Armstrong Woods Road reported 30 but only 5 verified. These 'untrapped potential' situations are the clearest priorities because we have direct evidence that cats are waiting."

---

### 10. "What locations have less than 70% alteration rate?"

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "This is where I have to be careful about what the data actually tells us. Some places show low percentages, but that doesn't always mean unfixed cats - sometimes it means missing data from older records.

> The clearer signal is active requests with untrapped potential. When a caretaker says 'I have 45 cats' and we've only processed 22, that's concrete evidence. A place with 5.9% altered might just be 5.9% recorded - I can't always tell the difference without digging into the individual cat records."

## Data Transparency

### 17. "What don't we know about the cat population in Sonoma County?"

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "Honestly, quite a bit. We only know about cats that either came through our clinic or were reported to us. There could be entire neighborhoods with community cats that we've never heard about. Rural areas are especially tricky - a farm might have 20 cats but if they never call us, we have no data.

> Our numbers are what I'd call 'minimum bounds' - the actual population is likely higher. What we DO know well is the cats we've helped and their outcomes."

---

### 18. "Why might our numbers be different from what a caretaker reports?"

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "Great question - this comes up a lot. A caretaker might say 'I have 30 cats' but we only show 15. That usually means they're counting cats we haven't processed yet - maybe cats that are trap-shy, or new arrivals, or kittens that aren't old enough yet.

> Our numbers reflect verified clinic visits. Theirs reflect what they see at the food bowl every day. Both are valid - they're just measuring different things. The gap between them tells us how much work remains."

---

### 19. "Are there areas where we probably have cats but no data?"

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "Almost certainly. I look at cities or zip codes with very few places in our system and wonder - is that because there are no cats, or because we haven't done outreach there? Rural areas, wealthier neighborhoods that might handle things privately, agricultural properties - these are all likely gaps.

> Low data doesn't mean low cats. It might just mean low awareness of FFSC or ability to access our services. That's where targeted outreach could make a big difference."

## Forward Looking

### 20. "Based on our data, what should FFSC prioritize to have the biggest impact?"

**Tippy's Response Style:**
> "Three things stand out:

> First, complete the active requests with untrapped potential - places like Annapolis Road where we KNOW cats are waiting. These are quick wins because someone is already engaged and ready to help.

> Second, follow up on places with recent activity but incomplete work. A colony that had 10 cats done last month might have 5 more ready now.

> Third, be careful about chasing 'low rate' places that might just be data gaps. Before sending a trapper to a place showing 5% altered, let's verify those are actually unfixed cats versus just missing records.

> The goal is always: real impact on real cats, not just improving statistics."

## Tips for Presenters

- **Follow up naturally** - "Tell me more" or "Why?" works great
- **Watch the reasoning** - HOW Tippy thinks matters as much as the answer
- **Caveats show sophistication** - When Tippy notes data limitations, that's intelligence, not weakness
- **Story format** - Tippy talks about cats and places like a colleague who knows the territory
- **Live data** - Numbers reflect current Atlas state, so exact figures may vary

---

## Why This Approach Works

**Story > Statistics:** Board members remember "Pozzan Road - 24 cats in one day" better than "average cats per event: 3.2"

**Caveats Build Trust:** When Tippy says "I'm not sure that 5.9% rate is real - let me explain why" - that's more credible than blindly reporting numbers

**Reasoning Shows Value:** The question isn't whether AI can query a database. It's whether AI can THINK about cat welfare like an experienced TNR coordinator. That's what Tippy demonstrates.
